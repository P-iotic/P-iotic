Velaphi

Episode 1: Ubuya Njani?

They whisper stories about him under faulty streetlights, where amagundane move like ithunzi in the dark, and even the dogs seem to bark with a question mark.

Velaphi.

The name means "he who appears" — but no one agrees when or where. They say he walks the valley like it’s part of his skin. He’s scarred, dressed like nobody, moves like memory. No school paper, no badge, no title — but every corner knows him.

Some say he was born in the soil of this place — mobu, as the elders call it. Others say he is the soil. A guardian not chained to a gate, but to every broken tap, every street with running sewage, every pothole that swallows tires like mouths wide with hunger.

In this place, the municipality is a ghost. Promises come like the rain — heavy, loud, then gone. Water goes. Lights go. Hope thins. But never dies.

He watches. He walks.

You could swear you saw him just now — the boy at the stop sign selling cigarettes, the man with sweets outside the spaza, that uncle drinking with the crowd, the pastor shaking hands, the bully who never really hit back too hard.

Grandparents say they knew him as a child. Batswadi claim he’s their age mate. My peers? They say he’s still young. No one knows. No one is sure.

Some say he’s felt before he’s seen.
When izulu elimnyama takes over the sky,
when the wind swirls and umoya ovusa umhlaba like abaphansi abanolaka,
they say: “He’s near.”
Not in voice — in presence.

And when izinyembezi zaQueen is cracked open,
not for joy, not for numbness,
but spilled slow into the dust,
it’s a cry.
A summon.
A prayer in liquor form.
Because sometimes, in the absence of power,
they ask the soil to bring their champ ekhaya.

There was a time I was a child, waiting to catch just a glimpse. Never sure if I had.
You see ithunzi move — maybe it was igundane, or maybe...

In this valley, people trade isiko for Western flash. Gold teeth instead of gold hearts. There’s WiFi now, but no trust. Hair straight, tongues twisted. Old ways sink into concrete.

And when Velaphi leaves for too long, amagundane begin to play.
They hesitate first. Whisper in corners. Throw threats like stones they can't carry.
But when they think he’s truly gone, they try to run this valley.
Until the wind shifts.
Until the sky darkens.
Until soil lifts.

And then — silence.

You never see him coming.
He doesn’t roar.
He doesn’t boast.
He just... is.

And the valley remembers its rhythm.

Because even the loudest mouths fall quiet when truth walks by —
and he walks softly, like the wind before a storm.

This isn’t his home.
This is him.

Protector of broken pipes and bent souls.
Warden of streets paved in rust and rhythm.
Ghost of discipline.

They speak his name only when necessary.
They disrespect him loudly in crowds — a performance.
But never alone.
Never face to face.

Because he’s the one who knows what changed while you blinked.
He’s the one who walks the whole place before stepping into his own yard.
He’s the one the dogs bark for in patterns.

And by the time you think of running,
he’s already behind you.
Or beside you.
Or maybe... he never left.

Velaphi — Kasi Folklore | Written in love for the soil
